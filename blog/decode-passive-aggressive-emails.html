How to respond to passive-aggressive emails at work

"Per my last email." "Just circling back." "As I'm sure you already know." A single line can hijack your whole afternoon. Here's how to decode the subtext and reply with calm clarity — without taking the bait.

Passive-aggressive emails work because they smuggle a complaint inside polite, deniable language. The words look professional. The feeling underneath is frustration, blame, or a status grab. Your nervous system reads the second part instantly — which is why a one-line message can leave you re-reading it ten times and drafting replies you'll never send.

The good news: once you can name the pattern, it loses most of its power. Here's a framework that works for almost any loaded message.

1. Name the move before you react

Most passive-aggressive emails are doing one of a few recognisable things:

  • The paper trail: "Per my last email" really means "I'm documenting that this is your fault."
  • The fake question: "Any update on this?" is pressure dressed as curiosity.
  • The status check: "As I'm sure you already know…" is a subtle reminder of who's senior.
  • The cold close: "Thanks." with a full stop and nothing else.

Labelling the move turns a vague threat into a specific, manageable one. You're no longer reacting to a feeling — you're answering a tactic.

2. Separate the ask from the attitude

Almost every passive-aggressive message contains a legitimate request buried under the tone. "Circling back again" still means "I need a date." Strip away the charge and you're usually left with something small and answerable.

Respond to the request. Ignore the jab. That single discipline ends most of these exchanges.

3. Reply short, warm, and specific

Long, defensive replies signal that the jab landed — and invite a longer one back. Instead, aim for three things: acknowledge, give a concrete next step, and stay genuinely warm. For example:

You receive: "Per my last email, I still haven't seen the deck."

You send: "Thanks for the nudge — sending it by 2pm today. Apologies for the wait."

No over-explaining, no matching their temperature. You've answered the real ask and quietly reset the tone.

4. Don't decode it alone at 9pm

The hardest part isn't writing the reply — it's the spiral beforehand. Reading subtext when you're already stressed makes everything sound worse than it is. That's exactly the moment to get a second, calmer read.
